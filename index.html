01 / Multi-agent automation

PO Automation Engine

A 5-agent pipeline that turns a vendor email into a verified delivery - no human re-keying in between. Built for 50–60 retail chains across 400+ delivery centres.

Discovery
  • Shadowed Supply Chain operators processing live POs
  • Walked the workflow with Sales and Supply Chain
  • Found every retailer's email arrived in a different format
Key insight

SAP wasn't the bottleneck. Interpreting the email before SAP could even be used - was.

Problem

50–60 retail chains (Zepto, Blinkit, Reliance, D-Mart) sent POs by email across 400+ delivery centres - each one manually read, validated against contracts, and keyed into SAP. The process took days during peak periods.

What I built

Defined the product workflow, aligned Sales, Supply Chain, and Finance on where automation could be trusted, and translated that agreement into five independently verifiable stages - ingestion, validation, order creation, scheduling, GRN verification.

Key tradeoff

Discrete agents over one end-to-end model. POs touch financial records, so a failed stage gets retried in isolation - not by reprocessing the whole order.

Lesson

The MVP relies on predefined mappings for known formats. V2 feeds exception resolutions back in, so the system adapts instead of staying rule-dependent.

Days → Minutes

02 / Enterprise analytics

Heritage Samarth

A self-serve revenue dashboard, scoped by role, so a Branch Manager, a Regional Head, and the CEO finally look at the same number when they say "yesterday's revenue."

Discovery
  • Compared MIS reports sent to BMs, Regional Managers, Zonal Heads, and the CEO
  • Found "yesterday's revenue" meant a different number to each of them
  • Every team had its own analyst manually reconciling numbers before a meeting
Key insight

It wasn't a data problem. Every stakeholder had quietly defined the same metric differently - they needed one agreed number, not more reports.

Problem

Sales performance lived in disconnected reports, each with its own assumptions. Leadership from Branch Manager to CEO needed one trusted, role-appropriate view - without waiting on an analyst to reconcile numbers first.

What I built

Audited how every team's MIS actually calculated its numbers, then ran alignment sessions with Sales to agree on one definition per metric - documented and enforced across systems, even where the underlying data still came from different sources. Samarth gives every level of leadership the same ₹6.6 Cr/day revenue picture, scoped to their role.

Key tradeoff

One governed metric definition over letting each team keep its own calculation logic. Slower to roll out - every team had to give something up - but it killed the "whose number is right" debate for good.

What surprised us

Getting four leadership levels to agree on one number took more negotiation than any model build. Once they did, adoption was immediate - nobody had to "double-check" a number that came from one governed definition.

03 / Procurement · Predictive

Heritage Prism

A weekly scorecard that tells procurement which collection centres and farmers need intervention before churn costs the business milk volume - built on the same governed data layer as Samarth.

Discovery
  • Procurement was tracking farmer churn risk manually, in spreadsheets, too late to act
  • V1 shipped as a flat KPI list - CXOs read top to bottom, not by theme, and missed what needed action
  • A red/green rule on "cost per fat kg" flagged a genuinely good outcome as a problem
Key insight

A flat KPI list told procurement what changed, not what to do about it. The fix wasn't a better chart - it was grouping by how procurement actually thinks: collection health → farmer health → who to intervene with.

Problem

Farmer and HPC churn risk was tracked manually in spreadsheets and surfaced too late for the field team to act. The first dashboard attempt listed every metric flat, with no signal for where to look first.

What I built

An RFM-based churn model ranking risk across 150+ collection centres, presented as a three-stage triage funnel - collection health, farmer health, intervene - instead of a KPI wall. Default view stays All India; zone is a drill-down, not the starting point, so procurement sees where to act before where to look.

Key tradeoff

Chose RFM over a heavier ML churn model - the business needed interpretable segments stakeholders would actually trust and act on, not just accurate ones.

What I'd reconsider

A flat red/green rule doesn't capture direction correctly everywhere - cost-up was sometimes a good outcome (driven by higher fat content), and "farmers per HPC" dropping could mean better service density, not attrition. V2 needs metric-specific direction logic, not one global rule.

04 / Geospatial · ML

Geospatial Territory Redesign

Clustering 900+ field agents across six states to find the milk volume nobody was capturing - and translating a model into a decision regional leadership would actually act on.

Discovery
  • Sales flagged markets with strong demand but weak performance
  • Mapped retailer, distributor, and field-agent locations across six states
  • Found territory allocation had grown organically, not from any structured plan
Key insight

Finding uncovered markets wasn't the hard part - any map could show that. Getting leadership to confidently approve acting on it was.

Problem

Distributor placement had grown organically over years. No one could confidently tell under-penetrated clusters apart from clusters that were simply under-served by territory design.

What I built

Turned an unanswerable sales question into a model leadership could act on - clustering field-agent and retail-coverage data across six states to surface real gaps, then building the revenue-backed case that took it from a chart to an approved distributor rollout.

Key tradeoff

Chose DBSCAN over fixed-radius clustering - retailers don't cluster in neat circles, and density-based clusters reflected real market behaviour while filtering outliers.

What I'd build next

Accurate clusters alone didn't drive adoption - leadership needed transparent reasoning and estimated impact before acting. Next: fold in distributor capacity, travel time, and serviceability constraints alongside geography.

05 / Consumer · Trust

Purity Ka Report Card

A QR code on a milk packet that lets any consumer verify the actual quality batch behind it. Conceived and shipped end-to-end: QA portal, public lookup site, and packaging integration.

Discovery
  • Heritage already ran rigorous lab testing on every production batch
  • None of that data ever reached the consumer after purchase
  • Mapped the customer journey instead of defaulting to "build an app"
Key insight

Trust wasn't missing because testing didn't exist. It was missing because customers had no way to reach information that was already sitting there.

Problem

Milk quality testing happened, but it was invisible to the end consumer - a trust gap with no product behind it.

What I built

Scoped this as a trust problem, not a tech problem, designed for the moment a consumer is deciding whether to buy: an internal QA batch-entry portal, a public lookup page, and a QR code on the packaging connecting the two - no app, no login.

Key tradeoff

Chose a QR-enabled public web page over an in-app feature - any smartphone camera could verify quality instantly, maximising accessibility at the point of purchase.

Lesson

Transparency alone didn't build trust - context did. Consumers wanted plain explanations, not raw lab values. Next: pair batch reports with freshness indicators and product education.

  • Every production batch covered
  • Live at the point of purchase
